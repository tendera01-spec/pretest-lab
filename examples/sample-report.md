# Sample Report

Anonymised example of a Synthetic Research Lab pass. Fictional brand "Solhaven", a wellness retreat in the Alps targeting professionals 40 plus. The pretest covers a single City-Light poster for an awareness campaign.

## Ad Pretest: Awareness Poster "Breathing Room" for Solhaven

**Verdict:** Ship with adjustments
**Score:** 3.4 / 5
**Channel/Goal:** City-Light poster, awareness, Munich city centre
**Dominant emotional system of audience:** Balance with Stimulance accent

### Archetype Panel

**Andrea, 47, Senior Project Lead in a consulting firm. Performer archetype, Dominance and Stimulance.** Sees the poster on her morning commute to the subway. "First thing I see is the mountain backdrop, then the word 'Breathing Room'. Nice composition, but within two seconds I don't know whether this is a yoga studio or a hotel. If I'm not standing still, I scroll past it in my head."

**Markus, 52, IT Director with three kids. Disciplined archetype, Dominance and Balance.** "Looks expensive. I assume it's a wellness hotel. The line 'When your calendar breathes' reads cleverly, but I don't know what to do with it. No prominent website, no date."

**Sabine, 44, Managing Director of a mid-sized company. Performer with Balance accent.** "This speaks to me. The calm in the image against the rush outside. The phrase 'Breathing Room' lands. I'd remember the name, but I don't think I'll google Solhaven tonight. The brand stays vague."

**Klaus, 58, tax advisor close to retirement. Traditionalist, full Balance. Skeptical voice.** "Another wellness hipster poster. Looks good, says nothing to me. What does it cost? Where is it? No info, no interest."

**Julia, 41, Marketing Director, athletic, single mother. Hedonist with Performer accent.** "Beautiful image. Makes me curious. 'Breathing Room' is a good phrase for someone like me. But when I see it again tomorrow I might remember the image, not the brand. The logo is too quiet."

### Framework Critique

**4.1 Visual Hierarchy:** 4 / 5. Clear first level through the mountain backdrop, second level headline, third level logo. Works. Logo could go one step stronger.

**4.2 5-Second Clarity:** 2 / 5. Weakest dimension. Anyone who doesn't know Solhaven doesn't understand what is being offered. Three of five personas guess or misread.

**4.3 AIDA:** 3 / 5. Attention yes (mountain backdrop catches the eye), Interest medium (clever but cryptic headline), Desire weak (no hook), Action missing (no clear next step).

**4.4 Message Clarity:** 2 / 5. "When your calendar breathes" is poetic but leaves open what is being advertised. Acceptable for an awareness campaign, but only if the brand anchor is strong. It isn't.

**4.5 CTA Strength:** 2 / 5. No CTA, only a small URL in the bottom right. For an awareness campaign that's acceptable, but the brand anchor would need to be stronger to compensate.

**4.6 Premium Perception:** 4 / 5. Imagery, typography choice and colour palette signal premium. Fits the audience and the implied price point.

**4.7 Brand Consistency:** 4 / 5. Based on visible brand signals, consistent. Logo fits the typography, colour fits the imagery. (No brand foundation document was provided to the test.)

**4.8 Limbic Resonance:** 3 / 5. Headline "Breathing Room" hits Balance (calm, safety), the mountain backdrop hits Balance plus some Stimulance (vastness, experience). Coherent for a Balance-dominant audience. However: no Dominance trigger, despite the audience containing a Performer share. Andrea and Sabine remain emotionally underserved.

### Creative Director Roundtable

**Through Paula Scher's lens on type and scale:** The headline lives at a reasonable scale but the logo retreats. Scher would push the brand mark itself to be a typographic act, not a corner credit. *Implication:* either lift "Solhaven" into the primary type hierarchy or commit to a brandless poster where the URL becomes the brand. Half measures don't carry.

**Through Erik Spiekermann's lens on hierarchy and craft:** Three competing focal areas (mountain, headline, microcopy block), no rhythmic relationship between them. The microcopy bottom right reads after the QR code, which inverts the trust-to-action sequence. *Implication:* tighten the type system. Microcopy left of QR, not right. Hierarchy from headline to subline to brand needs a defined rhythm.

**Through Phil Barden's neuromarketing lens:** Cognitive fluency is mid: the poetic headline forces System 2 engagement in a System 1 channel. Goal value is unclear, because the implicit goal ("calm in overwhelm") is present in the image but not in the offer. *Implication:* simplify enough that System 1 processes the offer in 2 seconds. The poetry can stay if a second-line offer translates it.

*These are stylised lenses based on documented principles, not original voices.*

### Concrete Fixes

1. **Make the brand visible.** Logo one step stronger, or a second brand element (the wordmark "Solhaven" above the mountain backdrop, subtle but readable from 5 metres).
2. **Add a sub-message.** Below "Breathing Room" a second line that hints at what Solhaven is. Example: "Retreat for the overworked. Alps." Brief, but solves the comprehension problem.
3. **Set a Dominance accent.** One word that picks up the Performer share. "Arrive instead of functioning" or similar. Makes the poster relevant for Andrea and Sabine without losing the Balance audience.
4. **Make URL/QR more visible.** Currently too small. Not legible at City-Light distance.

### What Works

- Premium feel sustained throughout
- Headline "Breathing Room" is linguistically strong, has recall value
- Visual composition is calm, stands out in urban context
- Colour palette differentiates from typical wellness advertising (no beige wash)
