# Expert Lens Principles

**Purpose:** Foundation for the Creative Expert Roundtable in Pretest Lab. Each lens with documented core principles, so assessments are grounded and not ventriloquised from intuition.
**Status:** Practitioner reference. Stylised lenses, not original quotes.

---

## Frame first

The roundtable lenses are not "what Ogilvy would say about your asset", but "what an asset looks like when filtered through Ogilvy's documented principles". This distinction must appear in the report, otherwise it slides into ventriloquism.

Sources: publicly published books, interviews, talks and casework of the named individuals. No fabricated original quotes. Lenses are tools, not authorities.

---

## Family 1: Creative Directors / Advertising

### David Ogilvy
**Source:** *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963), *Ogilvy on Advertising* (1983)
**Core principles:**
- The headline carries 80 % of the work. If the headline doesn't deliver, nothing does.
- Promise a concrete promise, not a mood.
- Facts sell, not adjectives.
- Clarity beats cleverness.
- Brand image consistent over time.
- Respect the consumer. Assumption: intelligent, forgetful, busy.
- Test, don't guess.

**When to activate:** Baseline for every asset. Always when headline quality, promise or clarity is the question.

### Bill Bernbach
**Source:** DDB casework (VW "Think Small", "Lemon", Avis "We Try Harder"), *Bill Bernbach Said...*
**Core principles:**
- Strip everything that isn't true.
- Self-irony and honesty win trust.
- Wit beats volume.
- "The truth isn't the truth until people believe you."
- Execution is strategy.

**When to activate:** Consumer brands, underdog positioning, cover letters where humility is currency.

### Mary Wells Lawrence
**Source:** Wells Rich Greene casework (Braniff Airlines, Alka-Seltzer, "I Love New York"), *A Big Life in Advertising* (2002)
**Core principles:**
- Brand as theatre. Make the brand a place people want to be inside.
- Boldness is a brand asset.
- Colour, scale, audacity. Don't whisper.
- Treat the audience as smart guests, not as a target.
- A pitch is a performance.
- Make it inevitable.

**When to activate:** Identity work, bold campaigns, decks that need to move a room.

### Stefan Sagmeister
**Source:** Sagmeister & Walsh, *Things I Have Learned in My Life So Far*, TED talks
**Core principles:**
- Style serves emotion.
- Hand, material, theatricality.
- Break conventions when they don't carry.
- Design should make you feel.
- Question the brief.

**When to activate:** When courage, emotional impact or breaking convention is the question.

### Dave Trott
**Source:** *Predatory Thinking* (2013), *One+One=Three* (2015)
**Core principles:**
- Say one thing.
- Order: Impact, Communication, Persuasion.
- Predatory Thinking: know the weakness of the competition.
- Direct, no frills.
- The brief is the brief.

**When to activate:** Direct response, pitches, competitive positioning, short read time.

## Family 2: Design & Typography

### Paula Scher
**Source:** Pentagram casework (Citi, Public Theater, MoMA, Tiffany, Windows), *Make It Bigger* (2002)
**Core principles:**
- Typography IS the image.
- Scale and confidence.
- An asset works in context.
- Identity as a system that flexes across scales.
- Colour and type carry the brand more strongly than logos.
- Mistakes are part of the process.

**When to activate:** Type-dominated posters, decks, identity work, scale and presence.

### Erik Spiekermann
**Source:** *Stop Stealing Sheep & Find Out How Type Works* (1993), MetaDesign, FontShop, Berlin BVG
**Core principles:**
- Type is craft.
- Legibility is not communication.
- Information design serves the reader.
- Grid and system over composition.
- Function over decoration.
- Public values.

**When to activate:** Type work, editorial, CVs, one-pagers, wayfinding, information graphics.

## Family 3: Persuasion & Behavior

### Robert Cialdini
**Source:** *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984), *Pre-Suasion* (2016)
**Core principles:**
- Six universal principles of influence: Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity. Plus seventh: Unity.
- The moment before the message matters as much as the message.
- Authority signals can override scrutiny.
- Social proof works hardest when similar others do the thing.
- Scarcity creates value, but fake scarcity destroys trust.
- Commitment shapes future behavior.

**When to activate:** LinkedIn posts, sales decks, CVs, cover letters, persuasion contexts.

### Phil Barden
**Source:** *Decoded: The Science Behind Why We Buy* (2013), building on Kahneman
**Core principles:**
- System 1 drives 95 % of decisions.
- Cognitive fluency.
- Goal value.
- Implicit reward beats explicit reward.
- Mental availability.
- Framing, anchors, choice architecture.

**When to activate:** Parallel to the Limbic lens. Almost always.

## Family 4: Presentation & Storytelling

### Nancy Duarte
**Source:** *Resonate* (2010), *Slide:ology* (2008), Duarte Design
**Core principles:**
- A great presentation is a story.
- Sparkline structure: oscillation between "what is" and "what could be".
- The audience is the hero, the presenter is the mentor.
- Big idea + visual + minimum words per slide.
- End with a "new bliss" vision.
- Open with a strong S.T.A.R. moment (Something They'll Always Remember).

**When to activate:** Pitch decks, investor presentations, any deck that needs to move a room.

### Chip & Dan Heath
**Source:** *Made to Stick* (2007), *Switch* (2010), *The Power of Moments* (2017)
**Core principles:**
- SUCCESs: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Story.
- Simple: find the core.
- Unexpected: violate a schema.
- Concrete: specific images beat abstractions.
- Credible: authority, anti-authority, statistics.
- Emotional: make them feel.
- Story: structure thoughts as narrative.

**When to activate:** Any messaging question, clarity and stickiness must coexist.

---

## Family 5: UX & Clarity

### Steve Krug
**Source:** *Don't Make Me Think* (2000, revised 2014), *Rocket Surgery Made Easy* (2009)
**Core principles:**
- Don't make me think.
- Scanability beats readability.
- Conventions exist for a reason.
- Mindless choices are good choices.
- Omit needless words. Then omit half of what's left.
- Usability testing is faster and cheaper than arguing.

**When to activate:** Landing pages, web copy, navigation-heavy assets, scanability conversion gate.

---

## Selection logic

Maximum three expert lenses per test plus Barden. More dilutes.

- **Pitch deck:** Duarte + Heath + Cialdini + Barden
- **Landing page:** Krug + Ogilvy + Barden. Optional Cialdini.
- **LinkedIn or X post:** Cialdini + Trott + Heath + Barden
- **Ad or poster:** Scher + Spiekermann + Ogilvy + Barden. Optional Sagmeister.
- **CV or cover letter:** Ogilvy + Spiekermann + Cialdini. Optional Heath.
- **Brand identity:** Scher + Wells Lawrence + Bernbach. Optional Sagmeister.
- **Email or newsletter:** Ogilvy + Trott + Heath + Barden

Three pointed observations beat five mediocre ones.

## Format in the report

Per lens two sentences:

1. Observation. What this lens sees here.
2. Implication. What that means for this asset.

No original quotes. "Viewed through Ogilvy's lens..." or "From Duarte's perspective on story-arc...".

Calibration note at the end of the roundtable: stylised lenses based on documented principles, not original voices.
